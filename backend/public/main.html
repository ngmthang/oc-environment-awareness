Local Environment Snapshot

140 coastal monitoring locations Source [1]

Orange County operational facts + course concepts (water scarcity, pollution pathways, biodiversity, climate, and solutions).

LOCAL METRICS

What OC reports track

  • Monitoring footprint: 42 miles of open coastline + 70 miles of harbor/bay frontage with 140 monitoring stations. [1]
  • Scale: about 7,500 water samples and 22,500 lab analyses per year. [1]
  • Water reliability: GWRS produced 101,051 acre-feet in 2024 and was on-line ~98.15% of the year. [2]

COURSE CORE

Why water is a big deal

  • Only 2.5% of Earth’s water is freshwater; most is locked in ice or groundwater, and surface water is ~1% of freshwater. [11]
  • Watersheds matter: land use upstream determines what reaches rivers, estuaries, and beaches. [11]
  • Wetlands provide “free services”: slow runoff, reduce flooding, recharge aquifers, and filter pollutants. [11]

COURSE CORE

Biodiversity = resilience

  • Biodiversity includes ecosystem, species, and genetic diversity; genetic diversity helps populations survive change. [12]
  • The current extinction rate is described as 100–1,000× the natural background rate; habitat alteration is the biggest driver. [12]

COURSE CORE

Climate adds pressure

  • Earth has already warmed about 1.1°C since the 1850s; additional warming increases coastal flooding, heat risk, and wildfire conditions. [13]
  • Air pollution also harms health (cardio + respiratory systems) and is regulated under the Clean Air Act; LA is highlighted as a major problem area. [14]

Ocean, Harbors & Water Supply

140monitoring stations · 101,051acre-ft recycled (2024)

Beaches/harbors monitoring + drought-resilient supply (advanced recycling + recharge) are the backbone of coastal health and water reliability.

CURRENT SITUATION

What we are doing well

Coastal monitoring & public notification

  • Structured monitoring and notification: when bacteriological standards are exceeded, the public is notified, signs are posted, and monitoring can increase; the report describes use of Beach Mile Days. [1]
  • Continuous response capacity: staff are on-call 24/7 for sewage or contamination incidents, with public information channels described in the report. [1]

Recycling & groundwater protection

  • Advanced treatment: membrane filtration, reverse osmosis, and UV with advanced oxidation/disinfection; delivery to recharge and injection components. [2]
  • Operational reliability: on-line about 359.2 days in 2024 (98.15%). [2]
  • Production reported as 101,051 acre-feet in 2024. [2]

RISKS

What the dangers are

  • Sewage spills: wastewater overflows can threaten waterways and beaches; untreated sewage contains high levels of disease-causing bacteria and viruses. [1]
  • Basin overdraft tracking: accumulated overdraft reported as 204,985 acre-feet (preliminary). [3]
  • Drought + warming pressures can increase demand while reducing imported supply reliability (snowpack timing and totals are less predictable). [7] [8]

EDUCATION

How it works

Beach postings

  1. Weekly sampling is performed at monitoring stations by multiple public agencies. [1]
  2. Exceedances trigger postings and potentially increased monitoring; Beach Mile Days compare extent and duration. [1]
  3. Public updates are provided through the report’s described channels. [1]

Why recycling matters

  • Purified recycled water supports recharge and helps protect against seawater intrusion. [2]
  • Monthly basin accounting (recharge, production, overdraft) is used to manage long-term sustainability. [3]

NEXT STEPS

What we need to do next

  • Maintain monitoring + rapid public notification to protect public health. [1]
  • Reduce upstream spill risks and contamination inputs that trigger postings. [1]
  • Expand drought-resilient supplies through recycling and recharge systems described in the annual report. [2]
  • Use basin accounting to guide sustainability decisions and reduce long-term overdraft. [3]
  • Reduce disposable plastics at the source to keep trash out of storm drains and the ocean. [11]

Flood Control & Beach Protection

+50% channel capacity increase Source [4]

Flood control reduces storm overflow risk; beach projects counter erosion; both interact with climate-driven extremes.

CURRENT SITUATION

What we are doing well

  • Channel modernization increased capacity by 50%. [4]
  • Beach erosion response: ~132,000 cubic yards of sand placed to restore targeted beach areas. [5]
  • Debris removal: ~357,000 cubic yards of trash/debris removed from roads and flood control channels. [5]

RISKS

What the dangers are

  • Remaining floodplains require future improvements to reduce storm risk. [4]
  • Heavier rainfall and stronger storm systems increase flood risk, especially when soils can’t absorb water after drought. [13]
  • Sea level rise amplifies coastal flooding during storm surge and high tides. [13]

EDUCATION

Why capacity + sediment matter

  • Higher channel capacity reduces overflow risk during storm events. [4]
  • Sediment from land development and erosion can fill waterways and worsen flooding; vegetation and buffers reduce erosion. [15]

NEXT STEPS

What to do next

  • Continue modernization projects that reduce floodplain exposure. [4]
  • Keep debris out of storm drains (litter is a pollution pathway to rivers/ocean). [11] [16]

Climate Context & Human Health

~200 mmsea level rise past century

Climate drivers change baselines (sea level, storms, drought). Health outcomes follow via heat, smoke, air pollution, and water quality.

PHYSICAL CHANGES

What is changing

  • Sea level: described as ~200 mm (8 inches) over the past century at example stations; El Niño can elevate levels. [6]
  • Global sea level rise is described as ~9.3 inches in the last 120 years, with additional rise projected by 2100. [13]
  • Warming amplifies extremes: droughts, heavier rainfall, and wildfire conditions. [13]

HEALTH PATHWAYS

How people are impacted

  • Air pollution affects cardiovascular and respiratory systems (heart disease, asthma, lung cancer). [14]
  • Wildfire smoke exposure is highlighted as increasing in Southern California. [15]
  • Water quality is a direct exposure pathway when contamination triggers advisories/postings. [1]

MITIGATION

Reduce the drivers

  • Key sources of greenhouse gases include burning fossil fuels, agriculture, and deforestation. [13]
  • Solutions: improve efficiency, switch to renewables, preserve/restoring forests and wetlands, and reduce waste. [13] [16]
  • Cleaner transport and energy reduce air pollution and protect health. [14]

ADAPTATION

Reduce the harms

  • Coastal adaptation includes restricting construction in high-risk areas, upgrading property, improving flood defenses, and restoring natural protection. [13]
  • Urban cooling (trees, shade) and exposure guidance reduce heat and smoke risk during extremes. [13] [14]

Ecosystems, Wildlife & Cultural Stewardship

Biodiversityhabitats · forests · fire · corridors

Healthy ecosystems need space (habitat), connection (corridors), and disturbance-aware management (fire ecology + cultural burning).

BIODIVERSITY

What biodiversity means + why it matters

  • Biodiversity exists at ecosystem, species, and genetic levels. [12]
  • It supports ecosystem services: water purification, climate moderation, nutrient cycling, pollination, and pest control. [12]
  • Main drivers of loss include habitat alteration/fragmentation, invasive species, pollution, overharvesting, and climate change. [12]

FORESTS + FIRE

Forests store carbon; fire is part of many ecosystems

  • Forests cover ~31% of Earth’s surface and store large amounts of carbon; protecting forests limits warming. [15]
  • Many forest/chaparral systems are adapted to fire; long-term fire suppression increases fuel buildup and can worsen catastrophic fires. [15]
  • Controlled/prescribed (and cultural) burns reduce fuel and can improve long-term outcomes compared with only reactive suppression. [15]

HABITAT + CORRIDORS

Fragmentation is a local issue

  • Fragmentation creates small, isolated patches with edge effects; corridors help populations persist. [12]
  • Example discussed in class: an OC Great Park wildlife corridor concept connecting regional habitats. [12]

INVASIVES + POLLUTION

Prevent introductions; cut pollution at the source

  • Invasive species often lack predators and can outcompete natives; prevention is the best control strategy. [12]
  • Pollution moves from sources → pathways → sinks; upstream prevention reduces ecosystem and human exposure. [11]

What To Do & Who Does It

5 R'srefuse · reduce · reuse · recycle · rot

Actions that matter: reduce pollution inputs, reduce waste, conserve water/energy, and support local agencies that do monitoring and infrastructure.

HOUSEHOLD ACTIONS

The 5 R’s + why “reduce” wins

  • The “5 R’s”: Refuse, Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Rot (compost). [16]
  • Recycling works best when people participate and buy recycled products; plastics are especially challenging. [16]
  • Reduce packaging + single-use plastics (bottles, bags, utensils) to cut waste before it exists. [16]

WATER

Conserve + protect water quality

  • Fix leaks, install low-flow fixtures, use drip irrigation, and reuse gray water where allowed. [11]
  • Prevent runoff pollution: fertilizers, oils, and trash from streets are a major source of water pollution. [11]

FOOD + SOIL

Protect soil; reduce chemical impacts

  • Soil erosion and over-fertilizing pollute water and air; cover crops, buffers, and reduced tillage help. [17]
  • Integrated Pest Management combines monitoring, biocontrol, and limited chemical use when necessary. [17]

AIR + CLIMATE

Cleaner energy, cleaner air

  • Vehicle emissions and fossil fuel burning are major pollution sources; solutions include electrics, renewables, and mass transit. [14]
  • Mitigation (cut emissions) + adaptation (prepare for impacts) are the two main response strategies. [13]

LOCAL AGENCIES

Who does what (Orange County)

  • Orange County Health Care Agency (Environmental Health Water Quality Team): coastal monitoring, postings, and public notification. [1]
  • Orange County Water District + Orange County Sanitation District: GWRS recycling and groundwater reliability. [2]
  • Orange County Public Works: flood control projects, channel upgrades, beach sand placement, and debris removal. [4]
